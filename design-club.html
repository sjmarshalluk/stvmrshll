Outside | Cross-Product Strategy | 2023–2024

Defining a Unified Onboarding Strategy Across Gaia GPS, Trailforks, and MapMy

Outside’s mapping ecosystem spans several independently built products—Gaia GPS, Trailforks, and MapMy—each with its own mental model, feature taxonomy, onboarding flow, and value story. As the organization shifted toward a unified subscription offering, it became clear that early user experience was fragmented and often worked against ecosystem-level goals.

I was asked to assess whether a single, universal onboarding system could accelerate activation, clarify value, and reduce redundant team effort across products. What became clear is that the problem was not merely inconsistent UI—it was a lack of shared strategy, ownership, and definition of what “onboarding” truly meant within a multi-product ecosystem.

The Strategic Problem

Through ecosystem audits, product interviews, and journey analysis, I surfaced several structural misalignments that prevented a centralized onboarding flow from being viable:

  • No shared value narrative. Products introduced themselves independently, often prioritizing upgrade prompts over first-use comprehension.
  • Different definitions of “activation.” Teams measured success differently, making cross-product onboarding impossible to standardize.
  • Brand architecture ambiguity. It was unclear whether onboarding should orient users toward the individual product or the broader Outside ecosystem.
  • Operational constraints. No central team existed to implement or maintain a unified system.

These findings reframed the initiative: the organization didn’t need a universal onboarding flow. It needed a unifying onboarding strategy that respected product realities while aligning teams around a consistent understanding of user value progression.

Reframing Onboarding as a Lifecycle, Not a Screen

A critical insight from Amplitude analysis and UX auditing was that onboarding was being treated as a one-time entry experience. In practice, users encountered onboarding opportunities repeatedly as they explored maps, searched for content, adjusted tools, or planned trips.

To shift this thinking, I defined a two-layer onboarding model:

  • Out-of-Box Experience (OOBE) — the initial value narrative, account access, and upgrade strategy.
  • Ongoing Onboarding (OO) — contextual education, triggered guidance, and reinforcement of core product value.

This structure created a shared language across teams and reframed onboarding as the process of helping users reach value—not simply moving them through a flow.

The Framework

The outcome was a modular onboarding framework that any product could adopt, regardless of platform or domain complexity. Rather than impose uniformity, it aligned teams around consistent principles while giving flexibility in execution.

1. Clarifying the User’s Path to Value

Each product needed to articulate:

  • Basic Value — the minimum behaviors required to understand how the product works.
  • Product Value — the actions that signal sustained usage and future retention.
  • Ecosystem Value — where cross-product opportunities naturally emerge.

This replaced siloed onboarding decisions with a structured, comparable understanding of what users must learn and when.

2. Multi-Scenario Onboarding

I introduced scenario-based onboarding paths based on entry point, subscription status, and prior Outside account history. This allowed onboarding to adapt intelligently to:

  • brand-new Outside users
  • returning users entering through a different product
  • premium or O+ subscribers with established workflows

3. Shared Structure, Product-Level Execution

The framework provided a common spine—value communication, intent capture, progressive education, and reinforcement—while giving each product latitude to define how those principles manifested in UI.

Product-Level Roadmaps

Although the initiative began as a cross-product system, what teams ultimately needed were clear, product-specific roadmaps that connected onboarding improvements to the broader strategy. I delivered prioritized next steps for each product based on their value moments, behavioral data, and technical maturity.

Gaia GPS

  • Recenter onboarding on benefits, not features.
  • Introduce value-based triggers for map and planning tools.
  • Fix lifecycle email gaps to reinforce learning over time.

Trailforks

  • Shift key explanations earlier in the journey.
  • Break long tooltip journeys into contextual steps.
  • Clarify permission prompts and safety features.

MapMy

  • Simplify account and upgrade decisions.
  • Align OOBE across platforms and reduce redundant education.
  • Clarify how MapMy fits into the broader ecosystem.

Outcomes

  • Created the first unified onboarding framework adopted across mapping teams, replacing inconsistent practices with a shared mental model.
  • Shifted the company’s approach from “build a universal onboarding flow” to “align on shared principles, then execute within product realities.”
  • Enabled parallel improvement by giving each product a roadmap independent of centralized resourcing.
  • Influenced broader organizational discussions around brand hierarchy, cross-product navigation, subscription clarity, and identity.
  • Established instrumentation priorities that allowed teams to finally benchmark value moments and activation behaviors.

While the project did not result in a single universal onboarding system, it delivered something more impactful: a shared strategic foundation for how Outside introduces value, reduces friction, and supports users across a complex product ecosystem.
