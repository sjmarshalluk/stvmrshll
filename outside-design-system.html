Reframing Design Systems as Experience Systems to Support Individual Products

Outside’s portfolio included Gaia GPS, Trailforks, MapMy, and multiple event and editorial products — each with its own onboarding, navigation, mapping tools, and UI patterns. Traditional design-system work wasn’t solving the real issue: inconsistent experiences that confused users and slowed teams down.

My role was to define a unified, cross-product experience architecture — a system that preserved product individuality while creating predictable, reusable experiences across the ecosystem.

The Problem

Every product team had its own UI library, patterns, and interpretation of core interactions:

  • Onboarding flows behaved differently across brands.
  • Map experiences (recording, following, layer management) varied wildly.
  • Event registration and account behaviors were inconsistent.
  • A traditional “one design system for all” model wasn’t viable — products had different audiences, tech stacks, and brand identities.

We didn’t need a larger design system. We needed a unified way to design experiences.

Key Insight: Components Weren’t the Problem — Experiences Were

In reviewing all Outside products, I decomposed them into three repeatable layers — a pattern clearly visible once the work was mapped across teams and use cases:

  • Common Experiences (e.g., record a track, follow a route, register for an event)
  • Experience Systems (patterns that shape the behavior and structure of those experiences)
  • Design Systems (visual styling and brand expression applied on top)

The “Deconstructed Design Systems” model I introduced clarified how these layers relate and where consistency should (and should not) be enforced.

The Experience System Architecture

Through synthesis of product reviews, team interviews, and analysis of shared user journeys, I introduced a hierarchical, interoperable system:

  • Global Experience System — the highest-level rules (e.g., destructive actions require confirmation modals). :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
  • Experience Systems for product families:
    • Core (Accounts, Onboarding, Navigation)
    • Maps (Recording, Layers, Following)
    • Events (Registration, Results, Check-ins)
  • Design Systems for individual brands (Gaia, Trailforks, MapMy, etc.)

This structure allowed experiences to stay consistent where it mattered, while still letting products express their brand, audience, and technical needs.

How the Model Worked in Practice

Each experience was defined with:

  • Interaction principles (e.g., what triggers a modal, how recording begins/ends)
  • Structural patterns (screen flows, states, error conditions)
  • Content rules (terminology, symbology, conventions)
  • Cross-product alignment requirements

Then each product’s design system skinned the experience system using its brand colors, typography, components, and tone of voice.

Outcomes

  • Created the first cross-product experience architecture at Outside.
  • Enabled product teams to build onboarding, map tools, and event flows using a shared experiential model.
  • Reduced duplication — teams built once and applied across brands.
  • Improved clarity in roadmap planning by separating “experience decisions” from “visual design.”
  • Provided a scalable foundation later integrated into the Maps Platform and Onboarding initiatives.

Most importantly, this reframed the role of design systems at Outside — from component libraries to experience infrastructure.
